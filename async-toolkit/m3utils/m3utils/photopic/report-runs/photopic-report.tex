\begin{abstract}
Government regulations on lamp efficiency (lumens per watt) and color quality
(Color Rendering Index) define a constrained optimization problem whose
solutions are not the smooth, broad spectra of incandescent bulbs but
narrow-band, spiky spectral power distributions.  We use \texttt{photopic},
a hybrid Modula-3/Scheme numerical tool, to explore the Pareto frontier of
this trade-off.  As the efficacy requirement increases, the optimal spectrum
converges to approximately three narrow peaks near 450, 540, and 610~nm ---
Thornton's ``prime color'' wavelengths --- while aggressively suppressing
power at all other wavelengths, including the red region critical for color
fidelity.  The results show that regulatory pressure, when pushed to its
logical extreme, drives light sources toward spectra that are visually
``weird'': they satisfy the letter of color rendering standards while
producing light that many people find unnatural.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

An incandescent lamp is a near-perfect blackbody radiator.  Its spectrum is
broad and smooth, rendering colors with perfect fidelity (CRI~$=$~100).
But most of its radiation falls in the infrared, giving it a luminous efficacy
of only $\sim$12~lm/W.  Paradoxically, incandescent lamps are excellent
\emph{radiant} emitters: a tungsten filament at 2700\,K converts roughly
85\% of its electrical input into radiation.  The problem is that at this
temperature only $\sim$5\% of the blackbody spectrum falls in the visible
band; the rest is infrared.  Government regulations worldwide --- EU
Ecodesign, US EISA/DOE, California Title~24 --- have mandated minimum
efficacy standards that incandescent lamps cannot meet, effectively banning
them.

The replacement technologies (fluorescent, LED) achieve high efficacy by
concentrating their emission in the visible spectrum.  But \emph{where} in
the visible spectrum?  The CIE photopic luminous efficiency function $V(\lambda)$
peaks sharply at 555~nm (yellow-green).  A monochromatic source at 555~nm
with perfect electrical-to-optical conversion would achieve the theoretical
maximum of 683~lm/W --- but it would render no colors at all.

Legislators recognized that pushing efficacy alone would produce light that
people hate, so they added color quality requirements.  The instrument they
chose is the CIE Color Rendering Index (CRI), a 1965-era metric based on
only 8~test color samples.  The regulatory question thus becomes:
\textbf{What spectral power distribution maximizes luminous efficacy while
satisfying the CRI constraint?}

This is a constrained optimization problem.  The constraints come from
regulations:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{CRI $\geq$ 80} (Energy Star, EU); CRI $\geq$ 90 (California Title~24 for some applications)
\item \textbf{CCT within $\pm$50K} of the nominal color temperature (e.g., 2700K for ``warm white'')
\item \textbf{Duv $\leq$ 0.012}: chromaticity tolerance (distance from the blackbody locus)
\end{itemize}

The central question of this report is whether the legislative response
actually solves the problem that people care about --- faithful color
reproduction --- or whether CRI's 8-sample design leaves enough room for
an optimizer to ``game the metric,'' producing spectra that pass the
regulatory test while rendering many real-world colors poorly.  We explore
what happens as you turn up the efficacy requirement and let the optimizer
find the best spectrum that satisfies CRI.  The answer is that the optimal
spectra become increasingly bizarre, and CRI fails to prevent this.

\section{Background}

\subsection{Luminous Efficacy}

Luminous efficacy of radiation (LER) is the ratio of luminous flux to radiant
power:
\begin{equation}
\eta = K_m \frac{\int_0^\infty S(\lambda)\, V(\lambda)\, d\lambda}
                {\int_0^\infty S(\lambda)\, d\lambda}
\end{equation}
where $S(\lambda)$ is the spectral power distribution, $V(\lambda)$ is the
CIE 1931 photopic luminous efficiency function, and $K_m = 683$~lm/W is the
maximum luminous efficacy at 555~nm.

For a 2700K blackbody, the total LER is only $\sim$15~lm/W because most
radiation is in the infrared.  If we restrict to the visible band
(380--770~nm), the visible-only LER is $\sim$88~lm/W.

\subsection{Color Rendering Index (CRI)}

The CRI quantifies how faithfully a light source renders colors compared to a
reference illuminant (a blackbody at the same correlated color temperature).
The calculation, following CIE 13.3-1995 and the colorimetric formulas in
Foley and van~Dam~\cite{foley-vandam}, involves:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Normalize the test spectrum to $Y=100$
\item Find the reference blackbody at the correlated color temperature (CCT)
      via search in CIE~1960 MacAdam $(u,v)$ space
\item For each of 14 Test Color Samples (TCS), compute the color shift
      $\Delta E^*_{UVW}$ using von~Kries chromatic adaptation
\item $R_i = 100 - 4.6\,\Delta E^*_{UVW}$ for each sample; $\text{CRI}(R_a) =
      \frac{1}{8}\sum_{i=1}^{8} R_i$
\end{enumerate}

The key subtlety: only TCS~1--8 contribute to $R_a$.  TCS~9 (saturated red)
is \emph{not} part of the official CRI score.  This creates a loophole that
the optimizer exploits.

\subsection{Thornton's Prime Colors}

In 1971, W.~A.~Thornton of Westinghouse showed that the most chromatically
effective wavelengths for white light are three narrow bands near
\textbf{450, 540, and 610~nm} --- the ``prime colors''~\cite{thornton1971}.
Conversely, wavelengths near 500~nm and 580~nm are ``anti-prime'' ---
they contribute power without adding chromaticity discrimination.
Thornton was named US National ``Inventor of the Year'' in 1979 for his
prime-color fluorescent lamp patents~\cite{thornton-inventor}.

The optimization results below independently rediscover Thornton's prime
colors from first principles.

\section{What Gets Regulated as a ``White Lamp''?}
\label{sec:scope}

Not every light source falls under efficiency and color-quality
regulations.  Colored lamps, decorative filament bulbs, stage lighting,
plant grow lights, and various specialty products are typically exempt.
The regulations target \emph{general-service lamps} --- the everyday
bulbs that replaced incandescents in homes and offices.  The precise
definition of ``in scope'' varies by jurisdiction and determines which
products face the efficacy/CRI constraints that drive the optimization
problem analyzed in this report.

\subsection{EU Ecodesign (2019/2020)}

The EU regulation defines its scope via a \emph{chromaticity boundary}
rather than an explicit CCT range.  A light source is treated as white
(and thus in scope) if its chromaticity coordinates satisfy:
\[
0.270 < x < 0.530 \quad\text{and}\quad
-2.3172\,x^2 + 2.3653\,x - 0.2199 < y < -2.3172\,x^2 + 2.3653\,x - 0.1595
\]
On the Planckian locus, $x = 0.530$ corresponds to approximately
1800\,K, so the regulation covers very warm lamps.  Sources outside
this boundary (UV, deep red, amber, grow lights) are exempt.
Quality requirements: CRI\,$\geq$\,80, with an exemption for
HID $>$4\,klm and outdoor/industrial applications.  There is no
$R_9$ requirement and no TM-30 requirement.

\subsection{US DOE General Service Lamp (GSL)}

The US defines scope differently: a lamp with CCT $< 2500$\,K is
classified as ``colored'' and falls outside the general-service lamp
definition under EPCA, exempting it from efficiency standards.
Lamps with CCT $\geq 2500$\,K and lumen output between 310 and
3300\,lm are in scope.  The current backstop (2022) requires only
45\,lm/W; the 2028 standard raises this to $\sim$125\,lm/W for a
typical 810-lumen omnidirectional lamp.  The DOE imposes
\emph{no color quality requirements at all} --- neither CRI nor $R_9$.

\subsection{California Title~24 JA8}

JA8 is the most stringent regime: CCT $\leq 4000$\,K (warm and
neutral white only), CRI\,$\geq$\,90, $R_9 \geq 50$, minimum
efficacy 45\,lm/W.  Products with CCT $> 4000$\,K are not JA8-eligible
for high-efficacy residential applications.

\subsection{ANSI C78.377 Chromaticity Standard}

While not itself a regulation, ANSI~C78.377 defines the nominal CCT
categories that manufacturers target and that regulations reference.
The 2024 revision added 1800\,K and 2000\,K for the first time,
reflecting the growing market for ``ultra-warm'' and ``candlelight''
LED products.  The standard now covers 10 nominal CCTs: 1800, 2000,
2200, 2500, 2700, 3000, 3500, 4000, 4500, 5000, 5700, and 6500\,K.

\subsection{The Regulatory War on Coziness}

Consumers choose low CCT for ambiance.  2700\,K is the standard
residential warm white; 2200\,K is marketed as ``warm dim'' or
``vintage''; 2000\,K and 1800\,K appear as ``candlelight'' or
``ultra-warm'' in restaurants, bedrooms, and hospitality settings ---
precisely the environments where people care most about how light
\emph{feels}.  The lowest CCT that falls under a binding efficiency
regulation is approximately 1800\,K (EU, via the chromaticity boundary)
or 2500\,K (US DOE).

Unfortunately, the physics of warm light works against efficiency.  The
warmer and cozier you want the light, the harder it is to produce
efficiently:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Lower theoretical LER.}  The visible-band LER of a
  blackbody drops from $\sim$109\,lm/W at 3000\,K to $\sim$88\,lm/W at
  2700\,K to $\sim$60\,lm/W at 2200\,K.  Achieving a given LER floor
  requires more aggressive spectral shaping at lower CCT.

\item \textbf{Higher Stokes loss.}  Matching the warm chromaticity
  requires substantial red phosphor emission ($>$600\,nm), which incurs
  the largest Stokes penalty in the Philips cost model: a photon at
  630\,nm costs $C = 630/(450 \times 0.90) = 1.56$ --- over 50\% more
  electrical power per optical watt than the blue pump.

\item \textbf{More demanding CRI reference.}  The CRI calculation
  compares the test spectrum against the blackbody at the measured CCT.
  At low CCT, this reference is very red-rich, so any deficit in deep-red
  emission is penalized more severely in the $R_i$ scores.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent Efficiency regulations therefore bite hardest on exactly the
products that serve the most atmosphere-sensitive applications.  A
restaurant owner who wants 2200\,K candlelight-quality LEDs faces a
tighter physics budget than someone lighting an office at 4000\,K ---
yet both must meet the same efficiency floor.

Of the major regulatory regimes, the EU is the most difficult to
satisfy at low CCT, because it is the only one that simultaneously
covers very warm lamps (down to $\sim$1800\,K), requires CRI\,$\geq$\,80,
\emph{and} imposes a high efficacy floor ($\sim$98\,lm/W system).
The US DOE has a higher efficacy target (125\,lm/W by 2028) but
imposes no CRI requirement and excludes lamps below 2500\,K.
California JA8 has the strictest color quality (CRI\,$\geq$\,90,
$R_9 \geq 50$) but requires only 45\,lm/W.  Only the EU squeezes
both ends --- efficacy and color quality --- across the widest CCT
range.

\section{The \texttt{photopic} Tool}

\texttt{photopic} is a hybrid application: performance-critical spectral
calculations (Planck's law, CIE color matching functions, numerical
integration) are implemented in compiled Modula-3~\cite{modula3,cm3}
libraries, while the optimization driver, CRI calculation, and plotting
are written in Scheme using MScheme~\cite{mscheme}, a Scheme
interpreter embedded in the Modula-3 runtime.  MScheme's \texttt{sstubgen}
tool automatically generates Scheme bindings from Modula-3 interfaces,
allowing the Scheme code to manipulate compiled M3 objects (spectral
arrays, integration routines, color-matching data) directly without
marshalling overhead.  This architecture provides the rapid prototyping
benefits of a scripting language for the optimization logic while
retaining compiled-language performance for the inner numerical loops.

\subsection{Spectrum Parameterization}

The optimizer works in log-amplitude space.  A test spectrum is represented as:
\begin{equation}
S(\lambda) = \exp\!\bigl(f(\lambda)\bigr) \cdot B_\lambda(T_0, \lambda)
\end{equation}
where $B_\lambda(T_0, \lambda)$ is the baseline blackbody at the target CCT,
and $f(\lambda)$ is a piecewise-linear function defined by $n$ parameters
(control points evenly spaced across 380--770~nm).

The resolution increases through \emph{hierarchical subdivision}:
$n = 2 \to 3 \to 5 \to 9 \to 17 \to 33 \to 65 \to 129$.  At each level,
COBYLA~\cite{powell1994} minimizes the negative efficacy subject to CRI, CCT,
and chromaticity constraints.  The solution from level $n$ is interpolated
to initialize level $2n-1$.

\subsection{Objective Function}

The optimization target vector passed to COBYLA is:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{t} = \begin{pmatrix}
-\eta \\
R_a - R_a^{\min} \\
R_9 - R_9^{\min} \\
R_{\min} - (R_a^{\min} - 10) \\
\text{CCT} - (T_0 - 50) \\
(T_0 + 50) - \text{CCT} \\
1000\,(0.012 - D_{uv})
\end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
where $\eta$ is the LER, and the remaining components are inequality
constraints ($\geq 0$ required).

to Thornton's prime colors:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{$\sim$450~nm} (blue): establishes the short-wavelength
  chromaticity anchor
\item \textbf{$\sim$540~nm} (green): near the peak of $V(\lambda)$ ---
  provides most of the lumens
\item \textbf{$\sim$610~nm} (orange-red): establishes warm color temperature
\end{itemize}

The progression is illuminating:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{2 parameters} (170~lm/W): Can only tilt the spectrum.  Shifts power

\item \textbf{3 parameters} (262~lm/W): First sign of spectral sculpting.  Blue

\item \textbf{5 parameters} (364~lm/W): Three-peak structure begins to emerge.

\item \textbf{17--33 parameters} (434~lm/W): Fully developed spiky spectrum.

\item \textbf{65--513 parameters}: No visible change.  Efficacy stable at
  434~lm/W to four significant figures.
\end{itemize}

The optimizer, given enough degrees of freedom, always converges to a
three-peak solution.  \textbf{This is Thornton's prime-color theorem,
rediscovered numerically.}  The convergence by 33~parameters
($\sim$12\,nm spacing) is notable: the optimal peaks are broad enough
that finer spectral resolution provides no benefit.

\subsubsection{Trust Region and Optimizer Artifacts}

However, the convergence is partly an artifact of the COBYLA trust
region schedule.  The hierarchical solver halves the trust region
radius (rhobeg) at each subdivision level: starting at 4 for 2
parameters, it reaches $\sim$0.008 at 513~parameters.  At high
resolution, this is too small for the optimizer to restructure the
spectrum --- it can only make tiny local adjustments, so the peak
shape is effectively frozen from level 4--5 onward.

To test this, we ran 513 parameters with a minimum trust region
of 2.0, preventing the radius from shrinking below this floor.
The result (Figure~\ref{fig:rhobeg}) is striking: the optimizer
finds a \emph{higher} efficacy (473 vs.\ 434~lm/W) but produces a
noisy, spiky spectrum with $\sim$29 peaks scattered across
500--650\,nm.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{fig_rhobeg_comparison.png}
\caption{Left: standard hierarchical result at 33 parameters (3
  clean peaks, 443~lm/W).  Right: 513 parameters with trust region
  clamped at $\geq$2.0 (29 noisy peaks, 473~lm/W).  The higher
  efficacy is an artifact --- the optimizer exploits fine-grained
  oscillations that happen to satisfy CRI\,$\geq$\,60 while
  concentrating power near the $V(\lambda)$ peak.  The result is
  physically meaningless: no real light source would have this
  spectrum, and $R_9$ degrades further to $-54$.}
\label{fig:rhobeg}
\end{figure}

\noindent This reveals a tension in the optimizer design:
\begin{itemize}
\item Too-small trust region: the optimizer cannot explore, and the
  solution is locked into whatever shape was established at low
  resolution.
\item Too-large trust region: the optimizer thrashes, producing noisy
  spectra that game the CRI metric without converging to a proper
  local minimum.
\end{itemize}
The clean three-peak solution at 33~parameters is not necessarily
the global optimum --- but the noisy 473~lm/W solution, while
technically feasible, is not a meaningful result.  The right
approach is likely a parameterization that directly represents
peak positions and widths, eliminating the mismatch between
spectral resolution and optimizer dynamics.

\subsubsection{Gaussian Peak Parameterization}

To test whether the peak widths are dictated by the CRI constraint
or imposed by the grid resolution, we implemented an alternative
parameterization: $K$ Gaussian peaks, each defined by center
wavelength $\mu_k$, width $\sigma_k$, and amplitude $A_k$, for a
total of $3K$ free parameters.  The spectrum is
$S(\lambda) = \sum_{k=1}^{K} A_k \exp\!\bigl(-(\lambda - \mu_k)^2
/ (2\sigma_k^2)\bigr)$,
evaluated on the same 391-point grid and fed to the same
CRI/CCT/efficacy constraints.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{fig_gauss_vs_grid.png}
\caption{Grid vs.\ Gaussian parameterization at 2700\,K.
  Top row: CRI\,$\geq$\,60.  The Gaussian parameterization (9~DOF)
  finds narrower peaks and achieves 454~lm/W vs.\ the grid's
  434~lm/W, confirming that the grid imposes unnecessary peak width.
  Bottom row: CRI\,$\geq$\,95.  Three Gaussians cannot produce the

\label{fig:gauss-vs-grid}
\end{figure}

\noindent At CRI\,$\geq$\,60, the Gaussian peaks are measurably
narrower than the grid peaks (blue $\sigma = 14$ vs.\ 15\,nm,
green 12 vs.\ 13\,nm, red 5 vs.\ 8\,nm), and the Gaussian
achieves 5\% higher efficacy.  This shows that the peak widths in
the grid parameterization are \emph{not} purely dictated by CRI ---
the grid resolution and the hierarchical warm-start impose some
minimum width.  However, the widths are close enough (within
$\sim$10\% for blue and green) that the CRI constraint is the
dominant factor.

At CRI\,$\geq$\,95, three Gaussians are insufficient: the
constraint forces broad coverage across the visible spectrum,
which a sum of 3 peaks cannot approximate well.  The grid
parameterization, with its 129 independent amplitude values, has
the flexibility to sculpt a near-continuous spectrum and achieves
51~lm/W more.

\subsubsection{The Limits of Thornton's Prime-Color Approach}

This result exposes a practical limit to Thornton's three-band
theory.  At moderate CRI ($\leq$80), three narrow phosphor bands
suffice and in fact outperform a broad spectrum --- fewer
conversion steps means lower losses.  But at CRI\,$\geq$\,95,
the spectrum must cover enough of the visible range to render all
8 CRI test samples faithfully, and three bands cannot do this.

In a real phosphor-converted LED, each additional phosphor
carries costs that our single-phosphor Stokes model does not
capture:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Compounded quantum efficiency.}  Each phosphor
  conversion step loses $\sim$10\% of photons (QE $\approx 0.90$).
  A second phosphor re-absorbing and re-emitting drops to
  $0.90^2 = 0.81$; a third to $0.73$.

\item \textbf{Parasitic reabsorption.}  Multiple phosphors in
  the same package absorb each other's emission.  A red phosphor
  ($\sim$630\,nm) can absorb green phosphor emission
  ($\sim$540\,nm), converting it with additional Stokes loss
  rather than letting it exit the package.

\item \textbf{Manufacturing complexity.}  Tuning 4--5 phosphor
  ratios to hit a chromaticity target with acceptable bin-to-bin
  variation is harder than tuning 2--3, increasing yield loss
  and cost.
\end{itemize}

\noindent The Philips cost model used in our Pareto sweeps
(Section~\ref{sec:philips}) treats each wavelength independently:
$C(\lambda) = \lambda / (\lambda_\mathrm{pump} \cdot \mathrm{QE})$.
This correctly captures the Stokes penalty for a single phosphor
conversion from the 450\,nm pump, but it does not account for
cascaded conversions or reabsorption losses.  The real-world
efficacy penalty for moving from CRI\,$\geq$\,80 to
CRI\,$\geq$\,95 is therefore \emph{larger} than our optimization
predicts, because the broader spectrum requires more phosphors
with compounding losses.  Thornton's three-band approach, by
minimizing the number of conversion steps, avoids these
penalties --- but at the cost of being unable to satisfy demanding
color-fidelity requirements.

\subsection{The R9 Problem}

The CRI score $R_a$ averages only TCS~1--8, which are relatively
unsaturated.  TCS~9 (saturated red) is reported separately.  When the
optimizer is free to set $R_9$ as low as $-100$, it exploits this:
the CRI~$\geq$~60 optimized spectrum at 17 parameters has $R_9 = -28$.

This means: under this ``optimal'' light, saturated reds look dramatically
wrong --- less red than under a reference illuminant.  A person wearing a
red dress would look dull; red paint would look brownish.  But the CRI
score says the light is acceptable.

This is the ``weird final state'' that regulations drive toward: a lamp
that passes the official color test while rendering an important class
of colors --- reds --- poorly.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{The Regulatory Paradox}

The results reveal a paradox in lighting regulation:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Regulations set a \emph{minimum CRI} (typically 80) and a
      \emph{minimum efficacy} (80--100~lm/W and rising).
\item The optimal solution for high efficacy at moderate CRI is a
      narrow-band, spiky spectrum.
\item Spiky spectra can achieve high CRI scores (by construction ---
      the 8 test colors are rendered well) while having poor real-world
      color rendering (especially saturated colors, TCS~9--14).
\item As efficacy requirements increase over time, manufacturers are
      pushed further toward spiky spectra.
\item The ``efficient'' lamp of the future satisfies all regulations
      but produces light that looks \emph{wrong} to many observers.
\end{enumerate}

This is not a hypothetical concern.  Commercial tri-phosphor fluorescent
lamps and phosphor-converted LEDs already exploit exactly this trade-off.
Their spectral power distributions, with 3--5 peaks and deep valleys, are
the market's practical approximation to the mathematically optimal solution
computed here.

As detailed in Section~\ref{sec:scope}, the EU sets CRI~$\geq$~80
with no $R_9$ requirement; our optimizer shows that this permits $R_9 = 21$
(Table~\ref{tab:tradeoff}).  The US DOE~2028 standard requires
$\sim$125~lm/W system efficacy but imposes no color quality floor at all.
Only California JA8 mandates $R_9 \geq 50$ together with CRI~$\geq$~90.

\subsection{Comparison with Thornton's Work}

It is important to acknowledge that Thornton was doing essentially the same
optimization we do.  His 1971 paper~\cite{thornton1971} describes
systematic variation of three-line spectra, evaluating CRI and a combined
figure of merit at each configuration --- this \emph{is} constrained
optimization, carried out with the computing resources of the early 1970s.
Walter~\cite{walter1978} made the optimization explicit, applying nonlinear
programming to a 31-variable discretized spectrum.

But we are asking a fundamentally different question.  Thornton asked:
\emph{What is the best white light?}  He found the prime-color wavelengths
and proposed better phosphor blends.  We ask: \emph{What happens when
efficiency regulations push lamp spectra toward Thornton's optimum ---
and keep pushing?}  Thornton's three-peak structure is the \emph{destination}
that regulatory pressure drives light sources toward.  Our contribution is
to trace that trajectory quantitatively: as the efficacy floor rises through
the range mandated by current and proposed regulations, we show how the
optimal spectrum evolves, where color fidelity breaks down, which color
samples are sacrificed first, and whether the regulatory metrics (CRI, TM-30)
actually detect the degradation.  Modern hardware lets us explore up to 129
degrees of freedom, sweep broad constraint ranges, and map entire Pareto
frontiers --- turning Thornton's insight into a detailed engineering forecast.

\subsection{What the Regulations Should Do}

The IES TM-30-15 standard addresses some of these problems by using 99 test
color samples instead of 8, and reporting both \emph{fidelity} and
\emph{gamut area}.  Mandating minimum $R_9$ scores (as some California
specifications do) partially closes the red-rendering loophole.  But the
fundamental tension between efficacy and color quality is a physical
constraint that no regulation can eliminate --- it can only choose where
on the Pareto frontier to operate.

\subsection{The Impossibility of Blackbody-Quality Light}
\label{sec:impossibility}

A common assumption in lighting policy is that consumers can
``buy whatever quality light they want, as long as the lamp meets
efficiency standards.''  This assumption is false.  We can show
quantitatively that \emph{no human technology enables a
blackbody-quality visible spectrum to meet government efficiency
standards at any warm CCT.}

\subsubsection{From spectrum to wall plug}

The luminous efficacy figures computed by \texttt{photopic} are
\emph{luminous efficacies of radiation} (LER): lumens per watt of
\emph{optical} power, determined solely by the shape of the spectrum.

  with today's best laboratory technology.  This fails every current

  only 68\,lm/W but also mandates CRI\,$\geq$\,90 and $R_9 \geq 50$,

  118\,lm/W --- still below the US DOE 2028 target of 120\,lm/W and the

from an LED cannot legally purchase it in the EU at any warm CCT, and
this situation will not improve even with foreseeable advances in LED
technology.}  The efficiency regulations do not merely encourage
manufacturers to optimize spectra --- they make blackbody-quality
illumination physically impossible to deliver at the required wall-plug
efficiency.

This is the fundamental policy trade-off that spectral optimization
quantifies.  Our Pareto frontier results (Sections~3--4) show precisely
how much color quality can be retained at each efficiency level.  But
the ``lossless'' ideal --- a smooth Planckian spectrum at warm CCT ---
is categorically excluded by the combination of LED physics and
regulatory floors.  The notion that energy-efficiency regulation is
``technology-neutral'' is contradicted by these results: the regulations
implicitly mandate non-Planckian, spectrally engineered light.

\section{Literature Review}

The optimization of white-light spectra has a history reaching back to
the 1950s, but two papers from the 1970s are particularly relevant to the
present work: Thornton's 1971 study of prime colors and Walter's 1978
application of nonlinear programming to lamp spectra.

\subsection{Thornton (1971): Prime Colors and the Structure of White Light}

W.~A.~Thornton of Westinghouse~\cite{thornton1971} posed the question:
\emph{What is the optimum spectral power distribution of artificial white
light?}  Starting from the CIE luminosity function $\bar{y}(\lambda)$ and the
IES--CIE color rendering index, Thornton evaluated white light composed
of three spectral lines.  By systematic variation of each wavelength while
holding the other two fixed (at 450, 540, and 610~nm), he showed that
these three wavelengths are local optima of both CRI and a combined
figure of merit $\text{FM} = 320\eta + \text{CRI}$.

Thornton's key findings:
\begin{itemize}
\item Three spectral lines at \textbf{450, 540, and 610~nm} --- the
  ``prime colors'' --- yield white light superior to commercial
  fluorescent lamps of the same chromaticity in \emph{both} luminosity and
  CRI.  Three-line light at 4200~K achieved $\eta = 0.58$, CRI~$=$~71,
  compared to $\eta = 0.48$, CRI~$=$~67 for a commercial cool-white
  fluorescent.
\item Wavelengths near \textbf{495 and 575~nm} are ``anti-prime'':
  they degrade CRI without contributing significantly to luminosity.
  Removing a slice of power near 500 or 580~nm from a cool-white
  lamp's spectrum actually \emph{raises} the CRI.
\item A 1966 demonstration with interference filters and projectors showed
  that 60 observers judged three-line prime-color light as providing
  ``very good, if not excellent'' color rendering of meat, vegetables,
  flowers, and skin tones.
\item The prime-color wavelengths correspond to the maxima of
  \emph{tristimulus difference functions}
  $\bar{b} = \bar{z} - \bar{x} - \bar{y}$,
  $\bar{g} = \bar{y} - \bar{x} - \bar{z}$,
  $\bar{r} = \bar{x} - \bar{y} - \bar{z}$,
  which Thornton termed ``bluminosity,'' ``verdinosity,'' and ``rubinosity.''
  He proposed corresponding units (blumen, verden, ruben) analogous to the
  lumen, and showed that rubinosity $\rho$ is an excellent predictor of red
  phosphor performance in tri-phosphor blends (Fig.~14 in the original).
\end{itemize}

Thornton's insight is that the prime-color wavelengths are not arbitrary
engineering choices but reflect the structure of human color vision: they
are the wavelengths that ``pull hardest'' toward their chromaticities per
watt of power, and are therefore the most efficient at establishing a
wide color gamut from a small number of spectral components.

\subsection{Walter (1978): Nonlinear Programming of Lamp Spectra}

Wolfgang Walter of GTE Sylvania~\cite{walter1978} took a fundamentally
different approach: rather than assuming a fixed number of spectral bands
and optimizing their positions, he applied \emph{nonlinear mathematical
programming} (the generalized reduced gradient algorithm of Lasdon et
al.)\ to find optimal spectra with \emph{no a priori constraint on shape}.
The spectrum was discretized into 31 variables (10~nm intervals from
400--700~nm), with the objective of maximizing luminous flux of a
40-watt fluorescent lamp subject to constraints on total UV excitation
power, ANSI chromaticity ellipses, and a CRI floor.

Walter's key results:
\begin{itemize}
\item Optimal spectra with CRI up to 90--95 consist of \textbf{just four
  narrow bands}, positioned at approximately 460, 530, 580, and 620~nm,
  stable across all four ANSI lamp types (cool white, white, warm white,
  daylight).
\item The unconstrained (CRI-free) optimum is a two-line spectrum (the
  MacAdam pair), confirming earlier theoretical work.
\item As CRI constraints increase from 50 to 95, additional bands
  appear and existing bands broaden, with a monotonic decrease in
  luminous efficacy (from $\sim$140~lm/W at CRI~50 to $\sim$107~lm/W at
  CRI~95 for cool white).
\item The optimization surface is ``fairly flat'' near the optimum:
  small perturbations to the SPD do not greatly affect luminance or CRI,
  suggesting practical tolerance for real phosphor emission profiles.
\item When \emph{all} component indices $R_j \geq C$ are required (not just
  the average $R_a$), efficacy drops further --- confirming that the CRI
  average conceals weaknesses in individual color samples.
\end{itemize}

The published discussion section is notable: Koedam and Opstelten of
Philips argued that the four-band optimum offers ``no real improvement''
over the Philips three-band lamp after correcting for realistic quantum
efficiency ($\sim$80\% rather than 100\%).  Thornton himself commented,
noting that if subjective criteria (color preference, visual clarity,
perceived brightness) are considered rather than CRI alone, three bands
may be superior to four.

\subsection{Relationship to Our Results}

Our \texttt{photopic} optimizer uses a different parameterization ---
continuous piecewise-linear log-amplitude perturbation of a blackbody,
optimized by COBYLA --- but solves the same problem Thornton and Walter
posed.  The three-peak topology that dominates our results at low CRI
floors (437~lm/W at CRI~$\geq$~60) corresponds to Thornton's prime
colors, while the broadening and addition of a fourth peak at higher CRI
floors (CRI~$\geq$~90 and above) matches Walter's four-band result.  The
wavelength positions we find ($\sim$450, $\sim$540, $\sim$610~nm) agree
with Thornton to within the resolution of our spectral parameterization.

That three independent implementations of the same optimization --- spanning
five decades and very different computational resources --- converge to the
same spectral structure is strong evidence that the prime-color peaks are a
genuine feature of the CRI/efficacy landscape, not an artifact of any
particular numerical method or parameterization.

\section{The Deficiencies of CRI and the Legislative Paradox}

\subsection{Why CRI is Known to be Wrong}

The CIE Color Rendering Index (CRI, $R_a$) has been recognized as
flawed since at least the 1980s, yet it remains the dominant regulatory
metric.  The principal deficiencies are:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Only 8 unsaturated test samples.}  $R_a$ averages $R_1$
  through $R_8$, all of which are low-chroma, pastel-like Munsell
  samples.  Saturated colors --- which are the ones most affected by
  narrow-band spectra --- are represented only in $R_9$--$R_{14}$, which
  are reported but \emph{not} included in the official score.  This is the
  loophole our optimizer exploits: it can drive $R_9$ (saturated red) to
  $-28$ while maintaining $R_a = 65$.

\item \textbf{Von Kries adaptation model.}  The CRI uses a simple
  diagonal (von Kries) chromatic adaptation transform, which is known to
  be a poor approximation of human color constancy, especially for
  narrow-band illuminants far from the blackbody locus.

\item \textbf{Uniform color space.}  The CRI computes color shifts in the
  CIE~1964 $U^*V^*W^*$ space, which is perceptually nonuniform.
  Equal $\Delta E$ values do not correspond to equal perceived color
  differences.

\item \textbf{Averaging hides problems.}  A lamp with seven $R_i = 95$
  and one $R_i = 25$ gets $R_a = 86$ --- an apparently acceptable score
  that conceals a severe deficiency in one color region.  Walter's work
  showed that requiring all $R_j \geq C$ rather than just $R_a \geq C$
  significantly changes the optimal spectrum and reduces achievable
  efficacy.

\item \textbf{Penalizes increased saturation.}  Because CRI measures
  \emph{fidelity} (closeness to the reference illuminant), a lamp that
  renders colors \emph{more} saturated than the reference --- which
  people often prefer --- receives a \emph{lower} CRI score.
  Thornton's color preference index (CPI) and the IES TM-30-15
  standard~\cite{tm30} address this.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Why Legislators Use It Anyway}

Despite these well-known deficiencies, CRI remains the basis for
lighting regulations worldwide (EU Ecodesign 2019/2020, US DOE/EISA,
California Title~24, Energy Star).  The reasons are partly institutional
inertia and partly the absence of a universally agreed replacement:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{IES TM-30-15/18} defines two metrics: $R_f$ (fidelity,
  similar to CRI but with 99 test samples and CAM02-UCS) and $R_g$ (gamut
  area, measuring saturation changes).  It is technically superior but
  has not yet been mandated by any major regulatory body.
\item \textbf{CIE~224:2017} proposed a general color fidelity index $R_f$
  similar to TM-30, but adoption has been slow.
\item The lighting industry has decades of CRI-referenced product data,
  specifications, and marketing materials.  Changing the metric would
  require re-characterizing the entire installed base.
\end{itemize}

The result is a regulatory landscape that \emph{incentivizes} the very
spiky, CRI-gaming spectra that our optimizer finds.  Manufacturers who
maximize efficacy while just meeting CRI~$\geq$~80 are rewarded by the
regulatory framework, even though the resulting light may render saturated
colors poorly.  The ``weird final state'' is not a theoretical curiosity
--- it is the market equilibrium toward which regulations push.

\section{Philips Phosphor Cost Model and $R_9$ Maximization Sweeps}
\label{sec:philips}

The luminous efficacy of radiation (LER) computed in
Sections~3--4 assumes unit quantum efficiency: every watt of optical power
in the spectral power distribution produces its full complement of lumens.
In a real phosphor-converted LED, shorter-wavelength photons from the blue
pump must be down-converted to longer wavelengths by phosphors, incurring
a Stokes loss proportional to the wavelength ratio.  A photon at 450\,nm
converted to 610\,nm loses $1 - 450/610 \approx 26\%$ of its energy as heat,
regardless of quantum yield.

Koedam and Opstelten of Philips~\cite{walter1978} proposed a simple cost
model that captures this loss: the cost of producing one watt of optical
power at wavelength $\lambda$ relative to the pump wavelength $\lambda_p$
is
\begin{equation}
C(\lambda) = \max\!\left(1,\; \frac{\lambda}{\lambda_p \cdot \eta_Q}\right)
\end{equation}
where $\eta_Q$ is the phosphor quantum yield (here assumed 0.95).  Photons
at the pump wavelength cost 1 (no conversion needed); longer wavelengths
cost progressively more due to the Stokes shift.  The ``Philips-corrected
LER'' replaces the radiant power denominator with the cost-weighted integral:
\begin{equation}
\eta_{\text{Phil}} = K_m \frac{\int S(\lambda)\, V(\lambda)\, d\lambda}
                               {\int S(\lambda)\, C(\lambda)\, d\lambda}
\end{equation}

We now invert the optimization problem.  Sections~2--3 maximized
\emph{efficacy} subject to a CRI floor; here we fix the efficacy floor and
ask: \textbf{what is the best possible $R_9$ (saturated red rendering) that
a spectrum can achieve?}  $R_9$ is not merely unconstrained --- it is the
\emph{optimization objective}.  The constraints are
$\text{CRI}(R_a) \in [\text{floor}, \text{floor}+2]$ (pinned to prevent
the optimizer from spending efficacy budget on CRI headroom rather than
$R_9$), $\text{CCT} \in [T_0-50, T_0+50]$, $D_{uv} \leq 0.012$, and
$\eta_{\text{Phil}} \geq E_{\min}$.  By sweeping $E_{\min}$ we trace the
Pareto frontier of $R_9$ vs.\ system efficacy.

(Table~\ref{tab:cri80-sweep}), meaning saturated reds look dramatically

efficacy.  Only explicit $R_9$ mandates --- as in California JA8's
$R_9 \geq 50$ --- can close this loophole.

\subsection{The Cheapest EU-Compliant Lamp}

The preceding analysis examines the Pareto frontier abstractly.  But there
is a concrete question with immediate market relevance: \emph{what does
the cheapest lamp look like that meets all EU~2019/2020 requirements?}

The EU Ecodesign regulation~(2019/2020) requires system efficacy
$\gtrsim$98\,lm/W and CRI\,$\geq$\,80 for general lighting, with no
constraint on~$R_9$ or any TM-30 metric.  For a lamp manufacturer
minimizing bill-of-materials cost, the dominant cost driver is the
number of LED chips and the amount of phosphor needed to produce the
rated lumen output.  Higher luminous efficacy means fewer chips and less
phosphor for the same lumens, so \emph{minimizing cost is approximately
equivalent to maximizing efficacy} subject to the regulatory floor.

Our optimizer answers this directly.  From Table~\ref{tab:tradeoff},
the maximum-efficacy spectrum at CRI\,$\geq$\,80 and 2700\,K achieves
412\,lm/W LER with:
\begin{itemize}
\item CRI\,$=$\,83 (just clearing the floor)
\item $R_9 = +21$ (saturated reds rendered at one-fifth fidelity)
\item Worst $R_i = 70$
\item A three-peak topology with deep spectral valleys
\end{itemize}

\noindent Applying the Philips cost model and production loss chain
(Table~\ref{tab:losses}), this corresponds to roughly 140\,lm/W
system at best-production technology --- comfortably above the
98\,lm/W EU floor.  The manufacturer has \emph{headroom} and would
likely operate below the theoretical limit, but the spectrum that
the cost incentive drives toward is clear: suppress power everywhere
except near Thornton's three prime wavelengths.

The $R_9 = 21$ is the telling number.  A consumer buying this lamp would
find that red fabrics, red paint, and skin tones under warm light all look
washed out compared to the incandescent it replaces.  The lamp passes every
EU test.  The CRI score of 83 might even appear on the packaging as a
quality indicator.  But the subjective experience is poor --- and the
regulation provides no mechanism for the consumer to know this in advance.

Under TM-30, this same spectrum would show reduced $R_f$ (since the
99-sample metric is harder to game) and a characteristically distorted
$R_g$ gamut shape.  Whether TM-30 adoption would change the cost-optimized
design point is precisely the question the Rf~sweeps in subsequent sections
are designed to answer.

\section{Grid-Based Integration Optimization}
\label{sec:grid}

\subsection{Motivation}

Each evaluation of the COBYLA objective function requires computing
CRI (14 color shifts), CCT (root-finding on the blackbody locus), LER,
and chromaticity --- a total of approximately \textbf{99 Romberg
integrations} per evaluation.  The breakdown:

\begin{itemize}
\item CRI calculation: 14 TCS $\times$ 3 tristimulus values (X, Y, Z)
  = 42 integrations for TCS color coordinates, plus 3 for the test
  illuminant's own XYZ, plus 3 for the reference blackbody = 48
  integrations
\item Chromaticity and LER: 4 integrations (X, Y, Z, total luminous flux)
\item Philips cost model: 1 integration (cost-weighted power)
\item CCT search: multiple iterations of the above during Brent's method
  root-finding
\end{itemize}

Each Romberg integration (Numerical Recipes \S4.4, midpoint rule variant)
uses adaptive refinement, typically evaluating the integrand at 50--200
wavelength points.  At 129 spectral parameters, COBYLA makes thousands of
objective evaluations over the 8-level hierarchical run, resulting in
optimization times of $\sim$50 minutes per sweep point.

\subsection{Approach: Pre-Tabulated Weight Vectors}

The key observation is that all integrands have the form
$S(\lambda) \cdot W(\lambda)$ where $S(\lambda)$ is the test spectrum
(which changes every evaluation) and $W(\lambda)$ is a fixed weight
function (CIE color matching functions, $V(\lambda)$, TCS reflectances,
Philips cost, etc.)\ that depends only on the problem setup.

By discretizing the visible range onto a fixed grid --- 391 points at
1\,nm spacing from 380 to 770\,nm --- we can:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Pre-tabulate all weight vectors $\mathbf{w}_k$ at grid points
  once during initialization
\item Sample the test spectrum $\mathbf{s}$ onto the grid (one pass
  through the M3 \texttt{ParametricSpectrum.Eval} function)
\item Replace each integration $\int S(\lambda) W(\lambda) d\lambda$
  with a dot product $\Delta\lambda \cdot \mathbf{s}^T \mathbf{w}_k$
\end{enumerate}

The pre-tabulated weight vectors include:
\begin{itemize}
\item CIE $\bar{x}(\lambda)$, $\bar{y}(\lambda)$, $\bar{z}(\lambda)$
  --- 3 vectors
\item $K_m V(\lambda)$ (photopic conversion) --- 1 vector
\item $C(\lambda)$ (Philips cost) --- 1 vector
\item 14 TCS reflectances $\times$ $\bar{x}, \bar{y}, \bar{z}$
  --- 42 pre-multiplied vectors of the form
  $\text{TCS}_j(\lambda) \cdot \bar{x}(\lambda)$
\end{itemize}

Each ``integration'' becomes a vector dot product in $\mathbb{R}^{391}$
--- a tight loop of 391 multiply-accumulates.  The speedup comes from
eliminating Romberg's adaptive refinement: where the adaptive method
evaluates the integrand at 50--200 points with convergence testing per
integration, the grid method does a single fixed-length dot product.
(The M3 Romberg code is compiled native code and runs fast; the win
is algorithmic, not a language-boundary issue.)

\subsection{Pure-Scheme Blackbody}

The reference illuminant (blackbody at the CCT) must also be computed
on the grid.  Rather than making 391 calls to the M3
\texttt{Blackbody.PlanckRadiance} function, we implemented Planck's law
directly in Scheme:
\begin{equation}
B_\lambda(T,\lambda) = \frac{2hc^2}{\lambda^5}
  \cdot \frac{1}{\exp(hc/\lambda k_B T) - 1}
\end{equation}
where $h$, $c$, and $k_B$ are defined as Scheme constants.  This
avoids 391 individual M3 function calls for the reference blackbody,
though the dominant savings come from the dot-product integration
method rather than from reducing cross-language calls.

\subsection{Implementation}

The grid mode is activated by the \texttt{-grid} command-line flag and
can be toggled at runtime via the \texttt{*use-grid*} variable.  This
allows direct comparison of results between the Romberg and grid methods
on the same optimization problem.

A critical implementation detail is the normalization convention:
the CIE CRI calculation normalizes spectra such that $X + Y + Z = 100$,
not $Y = 100$.  The \texttt{photopic} codebase follows the convention
that \texttt{xyz->Yxy} returns $(X+Y+Z, x, y)$ as its first element
(confusingly named ``Y'' in the function name but actually the sum).
Getting this wrong in the grid normalization caused CRI errors of up to
63 units in $R_9$ during development --- a subtle but critical bug.

\section{TM-30: A Better Color Metric}
\label{sec:tm30}

\subsection{Why CRI Needs Replacement}

The deficiencies of CRI enumerated in Section~7 are not merely academic:
our optimization results demonstrate that they have practical consequences.
The CRI loophole ($R_9$ not included in $R_a$) allows the optimizer to
produce spectra with $R_9 = -28$ while claiming CRI\,$=$\,65.  The
8-sample average hides severe rendering failures.  The von~Kries
adaptation model and CIE~1964 $U^*V^*W^*$ color space are outdated by
decades of color science research.

The IES TM-30-18 standard~\cite{tm30} was developed specifically to
address these problems.  It is not merely an incremental improvement ---
it represents a fundamentally different approach to characterizing color
rendition.

\subsection{TM-30 Architecture}

TM-30 evaluates light sources using \textbf{99 Color Evaluation Samples}
(CES), selected by Royer et al.\ from a database of 105,000 measured
object spectra to span the full range of real-world colors including
saturated hues~\cite{royer2012}.  Compare this to CRI's 8 unsaturated
Munsell samples (or 14 including the supplementary set).

The colorimetric pipeline replaces every component of the CRI calculation
with modern equivalents:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
\textbf{Component} & \textbf{CRI (1995)} & \textbf{TM-30 (2018)} \\
\midrule
Test samples   & 8 (unsaturated)        & 99 (full gamut) \\
Color space    & CIE 1964 $U^*V^*W^*$   & CAM02-UCS ($J', a', b'$) \\
Adaptation     & von Kries diagonal      & CIECAM02 (CAT02) \\
Scale          & Linear ($R_i = 100 - 4.6\Delta E$) & Logarithmic \\
Metrics        & $R_a$ (fidelity avg)    & $R_f$, $R_g$, $R_{cs,hj}$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\subsubsection{CIECAM02 Color Appearance Model}

TM-30 computes all color differences in the CIECAM02 color appearance
model~\cite{ciecam02}, which accounts for:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Chromatic adaptation}: The CAT02 transform (a $3\times 3$
  matrix applied in an LMS-like cone response space) models how the human
  visual system adapts to the illuminant white point.  This is
  substantially more accurate than von~Kries scaling, especially for
  narrow-band illuminants.
\item \textbf{Nonlinear response compression}: Cone signals are
  compressed by a power function modeling the saturation of neural
  response, parameterized by the adapting luminance.
\item \textbf{Perceptual correlates}: The model outputs lightness ($J$),
  chroma ($C$), hue angle ($h$), colorfulness ($M$), and saturation ($s$)
  as perceptual dimensions rather than tristimulus coordinates.
\end{itemize}

For TM-30, the CIECAM02 $J$, $a$, $b$ correlates are further transformed
into the CAM02-UCS (Uniform Colour Space) via:
\begin{align}
J' &= \frac{1.7 J}{1 + 0.007 J} \\
M' &= \frac{1}{0.0228} \ln(1 + 0.0228 M) \\
a' &= M' \cos h, \qquad b' = M' \sin h
\end{align}
This logarithmic compression yields a more perceptually uniform space
than the CIE $U^*V^*W^*$ used by CRI.

\subsubsection{TM-30 Metrics}

TM-30 reports three families of metrics:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Fidelity Index $R_f$}: Analogous to CRI $R_a$, but
  averaged over all 99 CES in CAM02-UCS.  The color difference
  $\Delta E'$ for each sample is mapped through a logarithmic function:
  \begin{equation}
  R_{f,j} = 10 \ln\!\left(\exp\!\left(\frac{100 - c_f \Delta E'_j}{10}\right) + 1\right)
  \end{equation}
  where $c_f$ is a normalization constant.  This ensures $R_f \leq 100$
  (equality only for a perfect match) and provides better discrimination
  at high fidelity than CRI's linear scale.
  $R_f = \frac{1}{99}\sum_j R_{f,j}$.

\item \textbf{Gamut Index $R_g$}: Measures whether the test source
  expands or contracts the color gamut relative to the reference.
  The 99 CES are assigned to 16 hue bins; $R_g$ is the ratio of the
  gamut areas (in $a'b'$ coordinates) of the test and reference
  illuminants, scaled so that $R_g = 100$ means identical gamut area.
  Values $> 100$ indicate gamut expansion (more saturated colors);
  $< 100$ indicates gamut contraction.  CRI penalizes both; $R_g$
  distinguishes them.

\item \textbf{Local chroma shift $R_{cs,hj}$}: For each of the 16 hue
  bins, TM-30 reports whether colors in that hue region become more or
  less saturated.  $R_{cs,h1}$ (the first hue bin, centered on red) is
  of particular interest: it directly measures the red chroma shift that
  $R_9$ only crudely captures.  Negative $R_{cs,h1}$ means reds are
  desaturated --- the precise failure mode our optimizer exploits.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Why TM-30 Matters for This Work}

The TM-30 metrics would fundamentally change the optimization landscape:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{No 8-sample loophole.}  With 99 CES spanning the full
  gamut, the optimizer cannot sacrifice one color region to boost
  another.  Saturated reds are represented by multiple CES samples,
  not just the single TCS~9.

\item \textbf{$R_{cs,h1}$ as a direct red metric.}  Rather than the
  blunt instrument of $R_9$ (which conflates hue shift, chroma shift,
  and lightness shift into a single number), $R_{cs,h1}$ isolates the
  red chroma shift.  This is precisely the quantity that determines
  whether reds ``look right.''

\item \textbf{Better adaptation model.}  The CIECAM02 transform handles
  the narrow-band illuminants produced by our optimizer more accurately
  than von~Kries scaling.  CRI may over- or under-penalize color shifts
  for these non-Planckian spectra.

\item \textbf{Gamut shape information.}  $R_g$ and the per-hue-bin
  metrics reveal \emph{how} colors change, not just \emph{how much}.
  A spectrum that expands the blue gamut while contracting red would
  show $R_g \approx 100$ (gamut area preserved) but $R_{cs,h1} < 0$
  and $R_{cs,h9} > 0$ --- information that CRI $R_a$ completely hides.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Implementation}

TM-30 is implemented in pure Scheme within \texttt{photopic.scm},
requiring no changes to the Modula-3 binary.  The implementation
comprises approximately 370~lines of Scheme code plus tabular data:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{99 CES reflectance spectra and CIE 1964 $10^\circ$ CMFs}
  (\texttt{ces99.scm}, $\sim$4300 lines): Extracted from the
  \texttt{colour-science} library~\cite{colour-science} (which
  provides the CIE 2017 dataset) and tabulated at 1\,nm resolution.
  The file also contains the CIE D-illuminant basis functions
  $S_0(\lambda)$, $S_1(\lambda)$, $S_2(\lambda)$ for reference
  illuminant construction.

\item \textbf{CIECAM02 forward model} ($\sim$90 lines): Full forward
  transform from XYZ to $(J, M, h)$, including CAT02 chromatic
  adaptation, Hunt--Pointer--Est\'{e}vez cone response space,
  nonlinear compression, opponent channels, eccentricity factor,
  and the complete lightness/chroma computation.  Viewing conditions
  use average surround ($c = 0.69$, $N_c = 1$, $F = 1$) with
  $L_A = 100$\,cd/m$^2$ and $Y_b = 20$.

\item \textbf{CAM02-UCS transform} ($\sim$8 lines): The
  $(J, M, h) \to (J', a', b')$ mapping with $K_L = 1.0$,
  $c_1 = 0.007$, $c_2 = 0.0228$.

\item \textbf{Reference illuminant selection} ($\sim$50 lines):
  Following CIE~224:2017, the reference is a Planckian blackbody for
  CCT~$< 4000$\,K, a CIE D-series illuminant for CCT~$> 5000$\,K,
  and a luminance-normalized blend for the 4000--5000\,K transition
  zone.  The D-illuminant is constructed from CCT via the standard
  daylight-locus chromaticity polynomials and the
  $S_D(\lambda) = S_0 + M_1 S_1 + M_2 S_2$ basis decomposition.

\item \textbf{Metric computation} ($\sim$80 lines): For each of
  the 99~CES, reflected XYZ values (via pre-tabulated weight-vector
  dot products, matching the grid pattern used for CRI) are
  transformed through CIECAM02 and CAM02-UCS, color differences
  are computed, and samples are assigned to 16 hue bins.  $R_f$ uses
  the logarithmic soft-cap formula, $R_g$ uses the shoelace polygon
  area formula, and $R_{cs,h_j}$ is the per-bin chroma shift.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Validation.}
The implementation has been validated against the \texttt{colour-science}
Python library (v0.4.7) on multiple illuminants: Planckian 3000\,K
($R_f \approx 100$, $R_g \approx 100$ as expected for self-reference),
FL2 ($R_f = 70.3$ vs.\ reference 70.1, $R_g = 86.4$ vs.\ 86.4),
and FL11 ($R_f = 80.1$ vs.\ 80.0, $R_g = 99.9$ vs.\ 101.1).
All results agree within $\pm 2$ units.  The CRI computation is
unchanged (regression-verified).

\paragraph{Performance.}
TM-30 adds approximately 30\% overhead to each grid-mode spectrum
evaluation, consistent with the pre-implementation estimate.
The 99~CES weight vectors are pre-tabulated at startup alongside
the existing 14~TCS vectors.

\section{TM-30 $R_f$ Maximization Sweeps}
\label{sec:rf-sweeps}

With the TM-30 implementation in place (Section~\ref{sec:tm30}), we
can now directly test the central hypothesis: does the ``CRI loophole''
--- three narrow peaks that game 8~test color samples --- survive under
TM-30's 99-sample color fidelity metric~$R_f$?

\subsection{Experimental Design}

We replicate the $R_9$ Pareto sweeps of Section~\ref{sec:philips} with
the objective function changed from ``maximize $R_9$'' to
``maximize~$R_f$'', keeping the same Philips phosphor-converted LED cost
model.  The optimizer sweeps Philips-corrected luminous efficacy floors
from 200 to 320~lm/W, finding the spectrum that maximizes $R_f$ at each
threshold while maintaining CRI$\,\geq\,$80 (the EU regulatory floor)
or CRI$\,\geq\,$95 (high-quality lighting).

The x-axis in our plots shows \emph{system (wall-plug) efficacy},
converting Philips LER via the production loss chain
(Table~\ref{tab:losses}):
\begin{itemize}
\item Best production: $\times 0.514$ (current top-bin parts)
\item Best lab: $\times 0.670$ (demonstrated lab results)
\item DOE limit: $\times 0.813$ (theoretical ceiling)
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Results: CRI\,$\geq$\,80}

Figure~\ref{fig:rf-frontier-both} overlays both CRI constraints.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{TM-30 $R_f$ is remarkably robust to efficacy pressure.}
  Even at CRI$\,\geq\,$80, the worst $R_f$ observed is 86 --- compared
  to $R_9$ values well below zero at the same operating point.

\item \textbf{The ``CRI loophole'' is invisible to $R_f$.}  The
  three-peak spectra that achieve CRI=80 with $R_9 < 0$ still score
  $R_f \geq 86$.  This is because $R_f$ averages over 99 samples:
  even if a few hue bins are poorly rendered, the overall fidelity
  remains high.

\item \textbf{$R_g$ exposes what $R_f$ cannot.}  The gamut jump at high
  efficacy (from $\sim$89 to $\sim$103) reveals spectral distortion that
  $R_f$ alone would miss.  This supports the TM-30 recommendation to
  report both $R_f$ and $R_g$ together, not $R_f$ alone.

\item \textbf{CRI$\,\geq\,$95 narrows the gap.}  With tight CRI, the
  optimizer cannot find loophole spectra, so $R_f$ tracks CRI closely
  ($R_f \approx 0.97 \times \text{CRI}$).
\end{enumerate}

These results confirm that replacing CRI with TM-30~$R_f$ in regulations
would not, by itself, prevent the efficacy-driven spectral distortion
documented in earlier sections.

\subsection{Per-Hue-Bin Analysis: Why Averages Hide the Problem}

Both $R_f$ and $R_g$ are aggregate metrics --- $R_f$ averages fidelity
over 99~samples, $R_g$ averages gamut area over 16~hue bins.  As such,
they are susceptible to the same averaging artifact that makes CRI~$R_a$
a poor indicator: localized failures in specific hue regions are diluted
by acceptable performance elsewhere.

TM-30 provides per-hue-bin fidelity values $\Delta R_{f,hj}$ across
16~bins (where $\Delta R_{f,hj} = 0$ is perfect fidelity).  Examining
these reveals the distortion pattern that $R_f$ alone conceals:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Low efficacy} (200~Philips~lm/W, CRI$\,\geq\,$80):
  All 16~bins are uniformly negative ($-0.02$ to $-0.10$), indicating
  mild desaturation across the board.  The worst bin (h14, red--purple)
  is $-0.103$.  The average $R_f = 89$ is a fair summary because the
  errors are uniform.

\item \textbf{High efficacy} (270~Philips~lm/W, CRI$\,\geq\,$80):
  The pattern \emph{reverses sign} in some bins.  Green--cyan bins
  (h7--h9) shift to $+0.05$ to $+0.09$ (oversaturated), while red
  bins (h1, h14--h16) shift to $-0.03$ to $-0.09$ (desaturated).
  Because positive and negative errors \emph{cancel in the average},
  $R_f$ reports~86 --- only 3~points worse than the uniform case.
  But specific object colors are rendered very differently from the
  reference illuminant: greens too vivid, reds washed out.
\end{itemize}

This sign-flip pattern is invisible to any averaged metric, whether
CRI~$R_a$ (8~samples) or TM-30~$R_f$ (99~samples).  A regulation
based on the \emph{worst} per-hue-bin value --- $\min_j R_{f,hj}$ ---
would be far harder to game, since the optimizer cannot compensate for
one bad bin by improving another.

\paragraph{The vindication of $R_9$.}
This analysis retroactively explains why $R_9$ (CRI special test color
TCS09, ``strong red,'' Munsell~4.5\,R\,4/13) is such an effective
canary for spectral distortion.  The red region is precisely where
three-peak spectra fail worst: the gap between the blue pump at 450~nm
and the red peak at 610~nm leaves deep reds and red--purples with
insufficient spectral support.  TCS09's reflectance straddles this
gap, making it maximally sensitive to the specific distortion mode
that efficacy-driven optimization produces.

As a single additional number alongside $R_a$, $R_9$ may be
more informative than $R_f$ --- it tests the \emph{weakest point}
of the spectrum rather than averaging over regions where the spectrum
performs adequately.  California's JA8 standard, which requires
$R_9 \geq 50$ alongside $R_a \geq 90$, appears well-targeted:
it directly constrains the failure mode that efficacy pressure creates,
without requiring the full TM-30 apparatus.

The ideal regulatory approach would combine both strategies:
TM-30's per-hue-bin breakdown (for comprehensive coverage) with a
hard floor on the worst bin (to prevent the averaging loophole).
Until TM-30 achieves widespread regulatory adoption, $R_9$ remains
a pragmatic and effective stopgap.

\section{Physiological Analysis: LMS Cone Excitation}
\label{sec:lms}

The colorimetric metrics analyzed so far---CRI, TM-30 $R_f$/$R_g$, and
individual $R_i$ scores---are all based on the CIE tristimulus framework,
which integrates spectral information through color matching functions.
These functions are \emph{linear} combinations of the true cone responses,
preserving chromaticity but discarding information about the spectral
distribution of cone stimulation.  This section goes deeper: we compute
the actual L, M, S cone excitations for several illuminants and analyze
how the spectral structure of LED light differs from blackbody radiation
at the level of individual photoreceptor channels.

\subsection{Method}

We use the Hunt-Pointer-Estevez (HPE) chromatic adaptation matrix
to transform CIE~1931 $\bar{x}(\lambda)$, $\bar{y}(\lambda)$,
$\bar{z}(\lambda)$ color matching functions into cone fundamentals:
\[
\begin{pmatrix} L \\ M \\ S \end{pmatrix}
= \mathbf{M}_{\text{HPE}}
\begin{pmatrix} X \\ Y \\ Z \end{pmatrix},
\quad
\mathbf{M}_{\text{HPE}} =
\begin{pmatrix}
 0.38971 &  0.68898 & -0.07868 \\
-0.22981 &  1.18340 &  0.04641 \\
 0       &  0       &  1
\end{pmatrix}
\]
This physiologically-based transform (used in CIECAM97s) gives
cone-like responses that are appropriate for chromatic adaptation
modeling.

Von Kries adaptation is applied by normalizing each cone channel
to the 2700~K blackbody white point:
$L_a = L / L_{\text{ref}}$, and similarly for $M_a$ and $S_a$.
After adaptation, a perfect blackbody match gives $(L_a, M_a, S_a) = (1, 1, 1)$.
Any deviation reveals a cone-level mismatch that the visual system
may detect, even when tristimulus values (and hence CRI) are well-matched.

We quantify the deviation using the root-mean-square cone contrast
(RMSCC):
\[
\text{RMSCC} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{3}\left[
\left(\frac{\Delta L}{L}\right)^2 +
\left(\frac{\Delta M}{M}\right)^2 +
\left(\frac{\Delta S}{S}\right)^2
\right]}
\]

\subsection{Illuminants Compared}

All illuminants are normalized to equal luminous flux ($Y = 1000$):

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{2700~K Planckian radiator} --- the reference.
\item \textbf{Waveform Lighting Centric Home A19} --- a commercial
  CRI~95+, $R_9 = 91$ LED (digitized from published SPD, adjusted
  to match datasheet chromaticity $x = 0.4598$, $y = 0.4106$).
\item \textbf{CRI\,$\geq$\,80 max-efficacy} --- our optimized spectrum
  with no $R_9$ constraint (highest LER).
\item \textbf{CRI\,$\geq$\,95 max-efficacy} --- same with tighter CRI.
\item \textbf{CRI\,$\geq$\,80, Phil.\ 250} --- Philips-corrected at
  250~lm/W (achievable system efficacy $\sim$129~lm/W).
\item \textbf{CRI\,$\geq$\,95, Phil.\ 250} --- same with tighter CRI.
\end{enumerate}

Table~\ref{tab:lms-contrast} reveals a dramatic finding.  The L and M
cone deviations are tiny ($<1$\%) for all illuminants---these long- and
medium-wavelength cones see similar signals from any spectrum that
matches the 2700~K chromaticity.  But the \textbf{S cone tells a
completely different story}: the efficacy-optimized spectra have
\textbf{45--55\% less S-cone stimulation} than the blackbody reference.

The commercial Waveform LED achieves only 3.7\% S-cone deficit---a
testament to its broad phosphor emission.  To put this in context: the
just-noticeable difference (JND) for the S-cone channel is typically
5--10\%, much larger than for L or M cones, because S cones comprise
only $\sim$6\% of the cone mosaic and are absent from the central
fovea~\cite{curcio1991}.  The Waveform's 3.7\% S-cone deficit is
therefore \textbf{at or below the threshold of visibility} for direct
illuminant comparison on a neutral surface---consistent with the
perceptual near-equivalence that high-CRI commercial LEDs achieve.

The visibility of this deficit depends critically on the viewing
condition.  In \emph{successive} comparison (one source at a time,
adapted to each in turn), the S-cone JND after adaptation is 5--10\%,
and 3.7\% would be near or below threshold.  But in \emph{simultaneous} comparison---both sources visible in the
same visual field, as when an incandescent and an LED illuminate
adjacent patches of the same wall---the situation changes dramatically.
Chromatic adaptation still occurs, but the visual system adapts to a
\emph{single} compromise state (roughly the spatial average of the
visual field), not independently to each illuminant.  Neither patch is
individually ``normalized away'' by adaptation; instead, both are
rendered through the same adapted gain, and their difference survives
as a spatial color signal.  Simultaneous chromatic discrimination
thresholds along the S-cone axis are approximately
0.5--1\%~\cite{krauskopf1992}, an order of magnitude lower than the
successive threshold, because the task is now spatial comparison rather
than comparison against color memory.  The Waveform's 3.7\% S-cone
deficit is therefore \textbf{4--8$\times$ the simultaneous JND}, and
should be \textbf{clearly visible} as a subtle color difference between
the two patches of wall.  The incandescent side would appear slightly
warmer (more yellowish-pink), the LED side slightly more neutral,
driven primarily by the S-cone channel.

Two additional mechanisms amplify the effect on real surfaces:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Deep red surface reflectance.}  Wall paint is not spectrally
  flat; if the wall has any reflectance above 650~nm (as most warm-toned
  paints do), the blackbody's 5.2\% deep-red L-cone contribution
  vs.\ the LED's 2.4\% creates an additional L/M ratio difference that
  depends on the specific surface.
\item \emph{Spectral metamerism.}  Surfaces with structured reflectance
  (wood, textiles, skin) may look quite different under the two
  illuminants even when the illuminants match on a neutral target.
\end{itemize}

The efficacy-optimized spectra produce S-cone deficits of
25--55\%---\textbf{far above any visibility threshold}, whether
simultaneous or successive.  Even the ``best'' case (CRI~95,
Philips 250~lm/W, 25\% deficit) is dozens of JNDs.  These spectra
would look unmistakably different from incandescent, despite matching
CRI and chromaticity: the S cone is being starved.

\subsection{Deep Red Contribution}

A key spectral difference between incandescent and LED sources is
the deep red region ($>650$~nm).  The 2700~K blackbody derives
\textbf{5.2\% of its L-cone excitation} from wavelengths above 650~nm,
and a small but non-zero contribution from $>700$~nm.  By contrast:

\begin{itemize}
\item The Waveform LED gets 2.4\% of L-cone from $>650$~nm (half the BB).
\item The efficacy-optimized spectra get $<0.5$\% --- essentially zero
  deep red contribution.
\item No LED spectrum provides \emph{any} power above 700~nm, while
  the blackbody's Rayleigh-Jeans tail extends indefinitely.
\end{itemize}

This deep red deficit affects the rendering of surfaces with significant
long-wavelength reflectance (wood, skin, brick, certain textiles).
Even after von Kries adaptation normalizes the overall L-cone gain,
surfaces that selectively reflect $>650$~nm light will appear
systematically desaturated under LED illumination.

\subsection{Cone-Weighted Spectral Distribution}

Figure~\ref{fig:lms-cone-signal} reveals \emph{where} in the spectrum
each cone gets its signal.  For the L and M cones, the blackbody and
LED distributions are broadly similar (both peak around 560--580~nm),
but the blackbody's deep red tail (shaded region $>650$~nm) provides
a contribution that no LED replicates.

The most striking difference is in the S cone: the blackbody provides
a broad, smooth signal from 400--500~nm, while the LED concentrates
its S-cone stimulation in the narrow 450~nm pump band.  Even though
the chromaticity matches (the integrated S response can be tuned
by adjusting pump intensity), the \emph{spectral distribution}
within the S-cone sensitivity range is fundamentally different.
This difference becomes visible when viewing surfaces with structured
blue reflectance---such as certain dyes, minerals, or artworks---where
the narrow pump cannot substitute for the broadband blue of a
thermal source.

\subsection{Five-Channel Receptor Analysis}
\label{sec:five-channel}

The three-channel (L, M, S cone) analysis above captures chromatic
differences but ignores two additional photoreceptor systems that
contribute to perception at typical indoor light levels:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Rod photoreceptors} --- sensitivity function $V'(\lambda)$
  (CIE~1951), peaking at 507~nm.  Rods are active at mesopic light
  levels ($\sim$0.01--600~cd/m$^2$) and contribute to brightness
  perception, spatial vision, and---through rod-cone interaction---color
  appearance~\cite{buck2004}.
\item \textbf{Melanopsin-containing ipRGCs} (intrinsically photosensitive
  retinal ganglion cells) --- sensitivity function $s_\text{mel}(\lambda)$
  (CIE~S~026:2018, Lucas et al.\ 2014), peaking at $\sim$490~nm
  \emph{in vivo}.  These cells drive circadian photoentrainment,
  pupillary light reflex, and---importantly---contribute to perceived
  brightness and spatial brightness constancy even at photopic
  levels~\cite{brown2012,lucas2014}.
\end{enumerate}

Both of these receptor systems peak in the 490--510~nm range---precisely
where phosphor-converted white LEDs have their characteristic spectral
valley between the blue pump ($\sim$450~nm) and the phosphor emission
onset ($\sim$520~nm).

We extend the analysis by computing five-channel adapted responses,
normalizing each channel independently to the 2700~K blackbody reference,
and computing a five-channel RMSCC:
\[
\text{RMSCC}_5 = \sqrt{\frac{1}{5}\left[
\left(\frac{\Delta L}{L}\right)^2 +
\left(\frac{\Delta M}{M}\right)^2 +
\left(\frac{\Delta S}{S}\right)^2 +
\left(\frac{\Delta R}{R}\right)^2 +

\right]}
\]

cone-only metric.

This result explains the perceptual observation that high-CRI LEDs,
despite near-perfect chromaticity matching, still ``feel different''
from incandescent light---an effect that the colorimetric framework
cannot account for.

\subsubsection{Mesopic Vision in Residential Settings}

The rod and melanopic deficits are not merely academic.  The 2700~K
warm-white LEDs that show these deficits are specifically designed for
residential evening environments: bedroom lamps, living room ambient
lighting, dining settings, and hospitality applications.  These are
precisely the conditions where \emph{mesopic vision} dominates.

At typical residential evening illuminance levels (50--300~lux at the
task plane), the corresponding retinal illuminance is well within the
mesopic range ($\sim$0.6--6~cd/m$^2$ for typical wall luminance at
20--30\% reflectance).  In this regime, rod photoreceptors are not
saturated and actively contribute to:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Brightness perception} --- mesopic brightness includes a
  rod contribution weighted by the CIE~1951 $V'(\lambda)$ function,
  which is exactly the channel showing a 23\% deficit.
\item \emph{Spatial vision and ambience} --- rods dominate peripheral
  vision and contribute to the overall sense of ``fill light'' in a
  room.  A 23\% scotopic deficit means the LED-lit room provides
  less peripheral rod stimulation than the incandescent-lit room at
  the same photopic lux level.
\item \emph{Color appearance via rod-cone interaction} --- at mesopic
  levels, rod signals feed into the chromatically-opponent pathways
  (the rod-bipolar/AII-amacrine pathway), influencing perceived hue
  and saturation~\cite{buck2004}.
\end{itemize}

The melanopsin-containing ipRGCs (26\% deficit) further affect
perceived brightness through their contribution to the pupillary
light reflex and to the irradiance-coding pathway that modulates
subjective brightness independently of the cone-based luminance
channel~\cite{brown2012}.

The irony is striking: the spectral valley at 470--510~nm that
enables efficient phosphor-converted white LEDs falls precisely on
the sensitivity peaks of the two receptor systems most relevant to
low-light comfort.  The ``cozy'' warmth of incandescent light may
owe as much to its rod and melanopic stimulation as to its chromaticity.

The CIE~191:2010 mesopic luminance model, applied to these S/P
ratios, predicts only a 3\% luminance deficit at 10~lux and
effectively zero above 50~lux, because at typical residential
wall luminances ($\sim$5~cd/m$^2$ at 200~lux on a 30\% reflectance
surface) the CIE model considers vision to be purely photopic.
However, the model was designed for road lighting and addresses
\emph{only} the luminance channel.  Rod photoreceptors are
physiologically active well above the CIE mesopic upper limit:
rods do not fully saturate until $\sim$300~cd/m$^2$, two orders of
magnitude above typical wall luminance.  Their signals continue to
contribute to spatial vision, peripheral ambience, and---through
rod-cone interaction~\cite{buck2004}---color appearance, through
pathways that the CIE mesopic luminance model does not capture.

\subsection{Implications}

This analysis reveals a fundamental limitation of all colorimetric
metrics (CRI, TM-30, individual $R_i$): they evaluate color rendering
through tristimulus integration, which is blind to the spectral
distribution of cone stimulation within each channel.  Two spectra
can have identical CRI and identical chromaticity, yet produce
dramatically different cone excitation patterns.

The three-channel S-cone deficit is concerning but physiologically
modest for high-CRI commercial LEDs (3.7\% for the Waveform).
The five-channel analysis reveals the true scale of the problem:
even the best commercial LED shows a receptor-level mismatch
seven times larger than cone-only metrics predict, driven entirely
by the rod and melanopic channels.

Three aspects of this finding are particularly significant:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Invisible to all existing metrics.}  CRI, TM-30 $R_f$,
  and all chromaticity-based measures are defined on the CIE~1931
  photopic observer ($V(\lambda) \approx 1.89L + M$).  They contain
  no rod or melanopic information.  A light source could have
  CRI~100, $R_f = 100$, perfect chromaticity, and still show 25\%+
  deficits in the non-cone channels.
\item \textbf{Worst where it matters most.}  The rod and melanopic
  sensitivity peaks (490--507~nm) align with the LED phosphor valley.
  The 2700~K CCT products with the largest rod/melanopic deficits
  are marketed specifically for mesopic residential environments.
\item \textbf{No amount of spectral shaping within the phosphor band
  can fix it.}  The pump-to-phosphor gap is a structural feature of
  phosphor-converted LEDs.  Filling it requires either a cyan emitter
  ($\sim$490$-$510~nm) or accepting the loss of pump-conversion
  efficiency that filling this spectral region implies.
\end{enumerate}

For the LED lighting industry, this suggests that a five-channel
receptor analysis should complement traditional colorimetric metrics.
A composite metric incorporating rod and melanopic channel contrast
would capture the physiological reality that current metrics miss,
and would specifically penalize the spectral valley that defines
phosphor-converted white LEDs.


\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:future}

Several directions for further work remain, building on the results
reported above:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{TM-30 as optimizer constraint.}  Section~\ref{sec:rf-sweeps}
  maximizes $R_f$ as the objective function while constraining CRI.
  The converse --- constraining $R_f$ while maximizing efficacy ---
  would reveal what regulations written in TM-30 terms would actually
  require.  A joint $R_f$/$R_g$ constraint (e.g., $R_f \geq 85$,
  $R_g \leq 105$) appears from our results to close the CRI loophole.
  The $R_{cs,h_j}$ per-hue-bin metrics could also be used as
  constraints to prevent selective desaturation of specific hue regions.

\item \textbf{Multiple CCTs.}  Extend the sweeps of Section~\ref{sec:philips}
  to the full range of commercial color temperatures (2700--6500~K).
  Walter showed that the four-band positions are remarkably stable across
  ANSI lamp types; verifying this with our continuous parameterization
  and Philips cost model would be valuable.

\item \textbf{LED-realistic spectra.}  Constrain the spectrum to
  physically realizable LED spectra: a narrow blue pump peak
  ($\sim$450~nm, 20~nm FWHM) plus one or more phosphor emission bands
  (50--100~nm FWHM, constrained shapes).  This would bridge the gap
  between our theoretical optima and practical LED design.

\item \textbf{Scotopic/mesopic optimization.}  Section~\ref{sec:five-channel}
  reveals that 2700~K LEDs show 23\% rod (scotopic) deficits versus
  blackbody, with the deficit concentrated in the 470--510~nm range.
  Adding a scotopic channel constraint to the optimizer---e.g.,
  requiring $|\Delta V'/V'| \leq 5$\%---would force spectra to fill
  the pump-to-phosphor valley at the cost of some luminous efficacy.
  This is particularly relevant for residential evening lighting,
  where mesopic vision dominates and rod contributions affect perceived
  brightness and ambience.

\item \textbf{Melanopic-constrained optimization.}  The 26\% melanopic
  deficit (Section~\ref{sec:five-channel}) is invisible to all current
  lighting metrics.  Adding a melanopic equivalent daylight illuminance
  (M-EDI) constraint following CIE~S~026:2018 would capture both the
  circadian dimension (melatonin suppression depends on melanopic
  stimulation) and the brightness/pupil dimension (ipRGCs modulate
  perceived brightness independently of cones).  The trade-off between
  visual efficacy and melanopic adequacy could be mapped with the same
  sweep methodology used for CRI and $R_f$ in this report.

\item \textbf{Optimizer robustness.}  The CCT comparison
  (Table~\ref{tab:cct-comparison}) reveals non-monotonicities where a
  tighter CRI constraint yields higher efficacy than a looser one,
  indicating that COBYLA finds different local minima at different
  constraint levels.  Three improvements would help:
  (a)~\emph{neighbor seeding} --- sweep CRI floors in order and use each
  solution as the starting point for the next, both upward and downward;
  (b)~\emph{multiple random restarts} per operating point, keeping the
  best result;
  (c)~\emph{monotonicity enforcement} --- if a tighter constraint
  achieved higher efficacy, re-run the looser point seeded from the
  tighter solution, since relaxing a constraint can never worsen the
  true optimum.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}

The constrained optimization of light spectra reveals that regulatory
pressure on luminous efficacy drives lamp spectra toward a mathematically
determined ``weird'' final state: three narrow spectral peaks near 450,
540, and 610~nm with suppressed emission elsewhere.  This state maximizes
lumens per watt while technically satisfying color rendering requirements,
but at the cost of poor rendering of saturated colors --- especially reds.

The five-channel receptor analysis (Section~\ref{sec:five-channel})
reveals a deeper problem: even well-designed commercial LEDs with
CRI~95+ show 23--26\% deficits in the rod and melanopic channels versus
blackbody, invisible to all existing colorimetric metrics.  These deficits
are concentrated in the 470--510~nm spectral valley that is a structural
feature of phosphor-converted white LEDs, and they fall precisely on the
sensitivity peaks of the photoreceptors most relevant to the mesopic,
residential environments where warm-white LEDs are deployed.  This
suggests that a complete characterization of light quality requires
going beyond the three-cone colorimetric framework to include at least
rod and melanopic receptor channels.

The \texttt{photopic} tool, built as a hybrid Modula-3/Scheme application,
enables rapid exploration of this trade-off space using COBYLA constrained
optimization with hierarchical spectral resolution.
